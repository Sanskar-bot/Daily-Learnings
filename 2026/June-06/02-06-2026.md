#### Why Hospitals Are Easy Targets

Hospitals are maximally data-rich but minimally security-prepared — they run outdated legacy systems they cannot take offline to patch, have no dedicated cybersecurity budgets or in-house security teams, employ overworked staff susceptible to phishing, and operate sprawling IoT networks of medical devices that each serve as potential entry points.

- **Legacy systems:** Windows XP-era software still runs critical medical equipment — vendors prohibit OS upgrades
- **No security budget:** Cybersecurity gets <6% of an already thin IT budget; money goes to equipment and salaries
- **No internal team:** Most government hospitals have no CISO, no SOC, and fully depend on NIC for all IT
- **Overworked staff:** Doctors and nurses are the #1 phishing target — one click compromised all of AIIMS
- **IoT attack surface:** MRI machines, ventilators, infusion pumps — all networked, rarely patched, easily exploited
- **Massive data sharing:** Labs, pharmacies, insurers, and government portals create dozens of third-party entry points

#### Why Ransomware Works Especially Well on Hospitals

Hospitals cannot tolerate downtime — a ransomware attack creates instant life-or-death pressure that forces rapid payment decisions, while weak backup culture means there is often no clean recovery path, leaving organisations with no choice but to pay or go dark for weeks.

- **Pressure to pay fast:** Patients die without access to records — no hospital can negotiate for weeks
- **Instant chaos:** Surgeries cancelled, ambulances diverted, ICU records gone — within minutes of attack
- **Backups on same network:** AIIMS had backups — attackers encrypted them first, eliminating all recovery options
- **Average ransom paid:** $1.27 million. Average downtime: 14 days. Healthcare attacks up 3x since 2020
- **59% of hospitals hit by ransomware in 2024** — the fastest-growing targeted sector globally
