#### Security Concepts Formalized for Report

**Section 1 — Foundational Theory**
- **Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) Attack** — formal definition: an attacker secretly relays and potentially alters communication between two parties who believe they are communicating directly
- **CIA Triad applied to this project:**
  - *Confidentiality* — broken in Acts 1 & 2, preserved in Act 3
  - *Integrity* — AES-GCM authentication tags protect against message tampering
  - *Availability* — session abort in Act 3 intentionally sacrifices availability for security (fail-closed design)
- **Kerckhoffs's Principle** — the security of a system should depend only on the secrecy of the key, not on the secrecy of the algorithm. Reinforces why open protocols (RSA, AES) are trustworthy.
- **Trust On First Use (TOFU)** — the model where a key is trusted on first contact and verified on subsequent connections. Explains why SSH warns on key change and why fingerprint verification matters.

**Sections 2–4 — Per-Act Technical Documentation**
- Each act section includes: setup steps, what the attack does (or doesn't do), Wireshark evidence, and a real-world parallel
- Learned how to write technical narratives that are precise but accessible

#### Real-World Attack Mappings Researched

- **DigiNotar 2011 CA Breach (Act 2 parallel):**
  - DigiNotar, a Dutch Certificate Authority, was compromised — attackers issued fraudulent SSL certs for Google, enabling large-scale MITM attacks on Iranian users
  - Maps perfectly to Act 2: the "trusted" public key was actually the attacker's key, and clients had no way to detect the substitution without out-of-band verification

- **WhatsApp Safety Numbers / Signal Safety Numbers (Act 3 parallel):**
  - Both apps implement exactly what Act 3 demonstrates — SHA-256 fingerprints of long-term identity keys displayed as human-readable strings
  - Users can compare these out-of-band to verify no MITM is present

- **SSH Host Key Verification (Act 3 parallel):**
  - First connection: SSH displays the host's key fingerprint and stores it in `~/.ssh/known_hosts`
  - Subsequent connections: if the fingerprint changes, SSH raises a stark warning — the same principle as Act 3's abort-on-mismatch

**Section 5 — Wireshark Side-by-Side Analysis**
- Wrote a comparative analysis of Act 1 plaintext packets vs Act 2/3 ciphertext packets
- Explained what each field in the TCP stream shows and what it proves

**Section 6 — STRIDE Threat Modeling**
- Applied the **STRIDE** framework to the system:
  - *Spoofing* — MITM impersonates both Alice and Bob
  - *Tampering* — MITM can modify messages (mitigated by AES-GCM auth tags in Acts 2 & 3)
  - *Repudiation* — no signatures, so either party can deny sending a message
  - *Information Disclosure* — the core attack in Act 2
  - *Denial of Service* — Act 3's abort is a controlled DoS to prevent data exposure
  - *Elevation of Privilege* — MITM gains access to private communications
- **Recommendations written:** TLS with CA verification, certificate pinning, safety number UX, end-to-end signing (not just encryption)
